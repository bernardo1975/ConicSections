    Section 2.2 Parabola in Sports

    In many sports, an object is "thrown": a ball, a discus, a hammer, a javelin etc. A shot is "put", and the long jump may be considered as "throwing one’s own body" as far as possible. Often the goal is to obtain the maximal distance, while sometimes a certain target must be hit (e.g., in darts, golf, basketball). Let us concentrate on the former situation here.
    By an ideal projectile motion we mean the motion of an object (or projectile) that moves along the path under the action of gravity only; the influence of friction, air drag or other aerodynamical influences is not considered (although they, in reality, play a significant role — as we have seen in Digression 4: Throwing a Ball).
    The following situation should sound familiar from what we have done during the semester: We launch the projectile from the height \(b\) with an initial velocity \(v_0\) at a launch angle \(\alpha\text{.}\) The horizontal and vertical components of the initial velocity are then \(v_0\,\cos(\alpha)\) and \(v_0\,\sin(\alpha)\text{.}\) There is only an acceleration in the vertical direction, downwards, due to gravity, given by \(-g\) where \(g\) is the acceleration due to gravity on the surface of the earth (given by the value \(9.81 \frac{\text{m}}{\text{s}^2}\)). The minus sign in \(-g\) signifies that the acceleration is downwards (towards the center of the Earth). Since the acceleration is the time derivative of the velocity, integration yields that horizontal velocity remains unchanged at \(v_0\,\cos(\alpha)\text{,}\) while the vertical velocity changes linearly as time progresses according to \(v_0\,\sin(\alpha) - g\cdot t\text{.}\)
    Integrating the horizontal and vertical velocities again with respect to the time \(t\text{,}\) yields the horizontal and vertical displacements at time \(t\text{.}\) The horizontal displacement is given by
    \begin{equation*} x(t) = v_0\,\cos(\alpha)\cdot t, \end{equation*}
    while the vertical displacement —taking the initial height \(b\) into account — is given by
    \begin{equation*} y(t) = v_0\,\sin(\alpha)\cdot t - \frac12 g\cdot t^2 + b. \end{equation*}
    Let us explore this projectile motion now in Python/SageMath: We define a function f that has arguments v0, alpha and t as well as b (the last one with a default value of 0). It should return the tuple (x,y), i.e., the \(x\)- and \(y\)-coordinates of the projectile at time \(t\text{.}\) Note that cosine and sine take angles in radians not degrees, so if you want to measure your angles in degrees you have to multiply \(\alpha\) by a factor of \(\frac{\pi}{180}\) in the above formulae.
    We can now explore the motion of your projectile for different values of v0 and alpha (we let b=0 for the moment). You can plot the path your projectile will take by using a parametric_plot. We fix a value of \(v_0\) and then by varying the value of \(\alpha\) find the angle where we obtain the maximal horizontal range (i.e., we find the value of $x(t)$ where we have $y(t)=0$, i.e., where the projectile returned to the \(x\)-axis).
    The picture above shows the parabolic paths of projectiles with the same initial velocity v0=10 but different angles (in steps of \(5^{\circ}\)). What distance do you get for which throwing angle \(\alpha\text{?}\)
    You should see that for a projectile to hit the ground (i.e., \(y=0\)) at a certain distance, you either have none, one or two

    angles for a given initial velocity (there is only one angle to achieve the maximal distance).
    In the above formula, we can make \(t\) the subject in
    \begin{equation*} x = v_0\,\cos(\alpha)\cdot t, \end{equation*}
    and then plug this into the formula for \(y\) to obtain
    \begin{equation*} y = - \frac{g}{2\,v_0^2\,\cos^2(\alpha)}\,x^2+\tan(\alpha)\,x+b. \end{equation*}
    Clearly, the right-hand side is a quadratic polynomial in \(x\text{,}\) and therefore the function \(y\) (now a function of \(x\) instead of \(t\)) is therefore indeed a parabola. So, alternatively to the above parametric_plot, you can also do a normal plot using this function — with the same result.
    Furthermore, we can now easily calculate the horizontal range, i.e., the value of \(x\) (greater than \(0\)) where the projectile returned to the \(x\)-axis (that is, where we have \(y=0\)): This is amounts to solving the quadratic equation
    \begin{equation*} 0 = - \frac{g}{2\,v_0^2\,\cos^2(\alpha)}\,x^2+\tan(\alpha)\,x+b. \end{equation*}
    for \(x\) and only considering the positive — larger — root. Remember that we have done something like this in one of the quizzes?
    The formula that one obtains for the horizontal range \(x_r\) is
    \begin{equation*} x_r=\frac{v_0^2}{g}\,\cos(\alpha)\left(\sin(\alpha)+\sqrt{\sin^2(\alpha)+\frac{2\,g\,b}{v_0^2}}\right). \end{equation*}
    For \(b=0\) this reduces to (using trig identities)
    \begin{equation*} x_r= \frac{v_0^2}{g}\,\sin(2\alpha), \end{equation*}
    and thus we obtain the maximal horizontal range \(\frac{v_0^2}{g}\) for the angle \(\alpha=45^{\circ}\text{.}\) Note that the initial velocity \(v_0\) enters quadratically in this maximal range — this is the reason why athletes practice so much to achieve their maximal "push" at the moment they are realeasing the object they are throwing!
    If the initial height \(b\) is not zero, then one can find the maximal horizontal range and the optimal angle by differentiating the above expression for \(x_r\) with respect to \(\alpha\text{,}\) setting it equal to zero and solving the equation obtained. Without going through the steps here, the maximal horizontal range \(x_{\max}\) is then given by
    \begin{equation*} x_{\max} = \frac{v_0^2}{g}\,\sqrt{1+\frac{2\,g\,b}{v_0^2}}. \end{equation*}
    The optimal angle \(\alpha_{\max}\) to obtain this maximal horizontal range is
    \begin{equation*} \cos(2\alpha_{\max}) = \frac{g\,b}{v_0^2+g\,b}, \end{equation*}
    or
    \begin{equation*} \alpha_{\max} = \frac12\,\arccos\left(\frac{g\,b}{v_0^2+g\,b}\right). \end{equation*}
    (Again note, the the angle here is given in radians, so you might want to multiply this result by \(\frac{180}{\pi}\) to obtain degrees.)
    Let us explore these formulae:
    • Check that for a fixed value \(b\gt 0\) the optimal angle \(\alpha_{\max}\) is an increasing function in \(v_0\text{,}\) but also less than \(45^{\circ}\) with \(\lim\limits_{v_0\to+\infty} \alpha_{\max} = 45^{\circ}\) (so, only for high velocities the optimal angle is close to \(45^{\circ}\text{.}\)
    • Check that for a given initial velocity \(v_0\text{,}\) the optimal angle \(\alpha_{\max}\) is a descreasing function in \(b\) (with \(\alpha_{\max}=45^{\circ}\) for \(b=0\)).
    • Also, fix a value of \(b\gt 0\text{,}\) and plot \(x_{\max}\) as a function of \(v_0\text{.}\) Do you observe that — except for very small values of \(v_0\) — this function is essentially a quadratic function? So again, the initial velocity \(v_0\) enters quadratically in the maximal range.
    Due to athlete’s technique and muscular system, aerodynamic effects on the object etc., the optimal angles will actually differ from the above optimal formula — achieving a higher initial velocity is usually better than achieving the exact optimal angle.
